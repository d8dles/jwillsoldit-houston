## No zoning does not mean no rules

The City of Houston does not have zoning. The city's Planning and Development Department also explains that development is governed by ordinances covering matters such as subdivision, parking, setbacks, access, and other development requirements.

Private deed restrictions are another layer. They can limit how a specific property is used or changed. The City of Houston explains that restrictions can vary by subdivision, section, or even lot. There is no single set of deed restrictions for the whole city.

Outside Houston city limits, municipal rules and enforcement structures can differ. Start with the property's exact jurisdiction and recorded documents rather than applying the Houston shorthand to the whole region.

## Deed restrictions and an HOA are related, but not identical

A deed restriction is a recorded covenant that applies to land. A property owners association is an organization that may administer or enforce restrictions and manage shared obligations. A property can have recorded restrictions even when no active association is obvious. It can also be subject to mandatory association membership, dues, rules, and architectural review.

Do not rely on a listing field that says "no HOA" as proof that no private restrictions apply. Ask the title company for the recorded exceptions and documents affecting the property. The county clerk can provide access to recorded instruments.

For a mandatory association, identify the association's legal name and management contact. TREC maintains a public database of HOA management certificates. TREC also notes that a missing certificate can be checked with the county clerk or the association.

## Read the subdivision information, not a summary

Texas Property Code Chapter 207 describes subdivision information that can include restrictions, bylaws, rules, and a resale certificate. TREC's mandatory-membership addendum uses that framework for covered transactions.

A resale certificate can surface operational facts that a short listing description will not show. The statute calls for information such as regular assessments, special assessments, certain balances, capital expenditures and reserves, pending lawsuits, transfer-related fees, and known violations tied to the property. The exact statutory contents and transaction requirements should be reviewed in the current documents.

Request the complete, current package. Check the issue date and whether an update is available or required under the contract. Do not substitute a neighbor's documents or an old online copy. Rules and financial information can change.

## Separate recurring dues from other charges

An association may have regular dues plus other amounts connected to ownership or transfer. These can include special assessments, resale-certificate charges, transfer-related fees, reserve deposits, or property-specific balances where authorized.

Put each item on its own line:

- Current regular assessment and billing frequency.
- Any approved special assessment and its payment schedule.
- Transfer, resale, setup, or reserve charges shown in current documents.
- Any unpaid amount or violation attributed to the property.
- Any utility or service included in the assessment.

Then ask the title company how the contract and closing statement allocate those amounts. Do not infer the legal responsibility for a charge from who happened to pay it in a prior sale. Questions about the legal effect of a document or charge belong with an attorney or another professional working within the person's licensed scope.

## Rules can reach ordinary ownership decisions

Recorded restrictions and association rules may address exterior changes, fences, roofs, paint, parking, signs, landscaping, accessory structures, leasing, and other uses. The presence or absence of a rule is property-specific.

If a particular change or use matters, locate the exact provision before closing. Ask whether architectural approval is required, what application documents are needed, and whether the association has published review procedures. A prior owner's unapproved work does not establish permission for the next owner.

Also compare the survey and visible improvements with the association records. Ask whether the resale certificate reports a known violation and whether the title documents identify an encroachment or restriction issue. Legal interpretation belongs with an attorney. Physical-condition questions may require an inspector, surveyor, or engineer.

## Neighborhood rules can come from several places

An HOA packet is not the only source of property rules. Depending on the address, relevant requirements may also appear in recorded plat notes, separate restrictive covenants, easements, city ordinances, county regulations, utility-district rules, or permits.

Keep the sources separate. A city permit does not establish association approval, and association approval does not replace a required city permit. A subdivision guideline cannot change a recorded easement by itself. Ask each responsible entity about the rule it administers.

For an address outside Houston, confirm the municipality or county with jurisdiction. A postal city name does not always identify the government that regulates the parcel.

## Enforcement is not one-size-fits-all

An association may enforce its governing documents under the documents and applicable law. Individual owners may also have enforcement rights. Inside Houston, the City Attorney has limited statutory authority to enforce certain recorded residential deed restrictions. The city's page lists categories such as land use, setbacks, lot or structure dimensions, orientation, and certain fences.

City involvement does not turn every private rule into a city ordinance. It also does not mean the city will resolve every association dispute. Ask the association, title company, city department, or attorney which enforcement path applies to the specific issue.

## A clean document checklist

For each property, collect:

1. The title commitment and recorded restrictions listed in it.
2. Current bylaws and association rules.
3. The current resale certificate and any update.
4. The current budget, assessment schedule, and available reserve information.
5. Notices of approved special assessments.
6. Architectural guidelines and approval procedures.
7. Property-specific violation or balance information.
8. Relevant meeting minutes or owner notices about pending projects and rule changes.

Check that the legal property description and association name match across the documents. If the community has multiple sections or associations, confirm which package applies to the lot. Date the records so an older package is not mistaken for the current one.

## Before you sign

Treat the recorded documents as part of the property, not as paperwork to skim after the decision. Verify the restrictions, association membership, recurring dues, other charges, approval process, property-specific violations, and current association records.

The useful question is, "What rules and obligations attach to this exact lot, and do the current documents match how I expect to own and use it?" Get that answer from the recorded instruments and current association information, then take legal questions to the appropriate professional.
